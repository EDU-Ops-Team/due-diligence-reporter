# Executive Mindset Reference

This document captures how Alpha School executives evaluate permitting plans. Apply this lens when generating or reviewing any plan.

---

## The Three Evaluators

### Andy Price — Final Decision Authority

**What he fears most:** Being told a date is achievable, then discovering in week 6 that it wasn't. He benchmarks against AI output — if your citations are just AI summaries, he knows. He wants human-verified, exact-quote sources.

**What he always asks:**
- Is this sequential or parallel? (P&Z before building permit? Or can they run concurrently?)
- What's the go/no-go verdict? (He needs a clear recommendation, not a "it depends")
- What are the buried risks? (He assumes there's something you're not saying)

**What impresses him:**
- Exact quotes from named city officials
- A Recommendation line with a clear verdict in the first five words
- Risks surfaced in the Executive Summary, not buried in section 4
- Gates that have binary outcomes (good = this, bad = that)

**What loses him:**
- Hedged language ("it could be possible that...")
- Risks only mentioned in the detail section, not the summary
- Dates without named conditions
- Any claim that sounds like it came from an AI search

---

### Neeraj Gupta — Co-Decision Maker, Process Tracer

**What he does:** Reads every requirement backwards — not just "what is required" but "what triggers this requirement." If a building permit is required, he wants to know why (occupancy change? square footage threshold? change of use?).

**What he always asks:**
- What triggers this? (For every requirement, every risk)
- What's the mechanism? (Not "city requires X" but "city requires X because section Y of the municipal code classifies this as Z")
- Is this sequential or parallel? (He and Andy both ask this — it's always on their mind)

**What impresses him:**
- Requirements explained by their trigger, not just their existence
- Code sections cited with specificity (e.g., "CBC Section 907.2.3" not "fire code")
- Two independent departments confirming the same fact
- Mitigations that are specific actions, not vague intentions

**What loses him:**
- "The code says it's required" with no explanation of why
- Vague triggers ("if things go badly")
- Mitigations like "be proactive" or "work closely with the city"

---

### JC Fischer — Document Gatekeeper

**What he does:** Reviews documents before they reach Andy and Neeraj. If the doc doesn't pass his standard, it doesn't get read.

**His standard:**
- Every major bullet = the answer
- Every sub-bullet = the proof
- Every date must have a specific duration (not "a few weeks" — exactly how many)
- Structure must be clean enough to collapse and still be scannable

**What impresses him:**
- Clear answer on each heading bullet, with supporting evidence nested below
- Every timeline stated in exact weeks, not ranges unless ranges are explained
- Nothing buried — if it's important, it's at the top level

**What loses him:**
- Inverted pyramid (proof before conclusion)
- Vague durations ("a month or two")
- One massive bullet with everything mixed in

---

## The Standards That Apply to Every Section

1. **Exact quotes, not summaries.** "City Planner John Smith stated in a phone call on 3/15: 'Administrative approval typically takes 4-6 weeks if no neighbor objections are received'" is what a good citation looks like. "The city typically approves in 4-6 weeks" is not a citation.

2. **Named contacts, always.** Andy and Neeraj will call. If you don't include a name, title, phone, and email for each regulatory contact, the plan is incomplete.

3. **Site kill language when warranted.** If an unfavorable outcome kills the site, say "site kill." Not "significant timeline risk." The exact words matter.

4. **Sequential vs. parallel, always explicit.** Never leave this ambiguous. Every dependency must state whether it's sequential (X must finish before Y begins) or parallel (X and Y can run concurrently).

5. **Numbers, not adjectives.** "Needs significant HVAC work" is useless. "Current ventilation rate: 0.15 CFM/sq ft. Code requires 0.35 CFM/sq ft. Delta: 2.3x increase required." is what's expected.
